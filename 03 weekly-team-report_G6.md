# Interview Transcript: Jia Hui (Tech Startup Founder)

**Interviewer:** Walk me through the last time you had to work overtime nonstop to keep your project on track.

**Jia Hui:** That was three weeks ago when our database crashed right before a major beta milestone. My six developers and I basically lived in the office for 48 hours, surviving on mamak delivery and iced coffee to rewrite the schema. With only about four months of runway left, missing that deadline meant we wouldn't survive our next funding round. I finally crawled back to my place in Mont Kiara at sunrise, completely dead on my feet.

---

**Interviewer:** What difficulties did you run into while sorting out employment terms when building your six-person startup team?

**Jia Hui:** Honestly, the biggest headache was balancing what talent actually costs in KL versus what my bank account could handle. To get decent senior devs to leave secure corporate jobs for a risky startup, you have to offer competitive salaries, but my runway is tight. I ended up having to sweeten the deals with equity stakes, which means a lot of complicated legal back-and-forth that I had to figure out on the fly while trying not to give away the whole company. It’s a constant stressor knowing their livelihoods depend entirely on me securing the next funding round.

---

**Interviewer:** Could you outline your typical workday tasks related to staff payroll and work scheduling?

**Jia Hui:** Payroll usually takes up my entire morning on the 25th of every month. I sit at my desk with a massive spreadsheet, manually calculating the EPF, SOCSO, and EIS contributions for my six staff before logging into the corporate banking portal to release the funds. For scheduling, we use a shared digital board where I review their sprint tasks every morning at 9:30 AM. A lot of my time is spent adjusting deadlines on the fly because if one dev gets stuck on a bug, it delays the whole release and burns through our cash faster.

---

**Interviewer:** How do you normally respond when your team members bring up concerns about work benefits and conditions?

**Jia Hui:** I try to listen and keep things as transparent as possible, but it’s tough when the budget is this tight. When they ask about better medical benefits or extra perks, I have to be honest and tell them that right now, every single ringgit is tied up in keeping the lights on and extending our runway. I try to make up for it by offering flexible remote hours, but the underlying pressure is always there. It’s hard looking your team in the eye and asking them to be patient while knowing exactly how much pressure they're under.

---

**Interviewer:** Tell me what you did the last time tight cash flow forced you to cut manpower costs for the business.

**Jia Hui:** It was late last year, around November, when a major client delayed their milestone payment, and I realized we wouldn't make payroll by January if I didn't act immediately. I had to sit down with our junior UI/UX designer and let him go—it was the hardest conversation I've ever had because he did nothing wrong, but his salary was the only flexible line item we had left. To cover his workload, I had to absorb all the design prototyping myself on top of coding and investor pitches. For the remaining five staff, I had to ask them to freeze all overtime claims and suspend our yearly bonuses, which felt terrible but it was either that or shut down the company entirely.

---

**Interviewer:** Which details make you struggle between hiring expenses and local employment rules during recruitment?

**Jia Hui:** The biggest struggle is that local statutory contributions like EPF and SOCSO add a hidden 13% to 15% on top of whatever base salary we agree on. When you're operating on a razor-thin runway, that extra overhead means hiring a senior dev at RM 9,000 actually costs the business well over RM 10,000 a month. Navigating the strict local labor laws also means I can't just easily adjust headcount if our funding stalls, so every single hiring decision feels like a massive, high-stakes financial gamble. I spend hours running numbers on spreadsheets late at night, trying to see if we can stretch our cash for another three months just to comply with these fixed costs.

---

**Interviewer:** How do you adjust arrangements when employees struggle to keep up with their workload?

**Jia Hui:** When someone starts falling behind, I usually pull them into a quick one-on-one at the cafe downstairs to figure out where the bottleneck is. I can't afford to let things slide because a delay from one person pushes back our entire product roadmap, which freaks out our investors. Usually, I'll take some of their non-essential tasks and absorb them myself, or I'll reassign them to another dev, but that just ends up stretching the rest of the team even thinner. It's a stressful balancing act because I don't want to burn them out, but our runway doesn't give us any room to slow down.

---

**Interviewer:** Aside from base salaries, what unexpected monthly costs do you cover for your team’s benefits?

**Jia Hui:** On top of the statutory stuff, the biggest recurring hit is our monthly cloud server bill, which spikes every time the team runs heavy staging tests, easily draining an extra RM 3,000 to RM 4,000 that we have to cover just so they can work. Then there's the daily office expenses in Mont Kiara—like keeping the pantry stocked with enough high-grade coffee and snacks to fuel those late-night pushes, plus reimbursing their Grab rides and dinners when they stay past 9:00 PM. It doesn't sound like much on paper, but when you're counting every single ringgit, those small operational perks add up to a heavy chunk of cash flowing out of our account each month.

---

**Interviewer:** What follow-up issues popped up after you cut your team’s downtime to rush a tight deadline last time?

**Jia Hui:** The biggest issue was a massive wave of burnout and resentment that lingered for weeks. Because everyone was exhausted from working around the clock, they started making sloppy coding mistakes, which meant we spent the next fortnight fixing bugs instead of actually moving forward. Morale dropped so low that the office was completely silent, and people started passive-aggressively logging off at exactly 6:00 PM. On top of that, I ended up having severe chest tightness and couldn't sleep for days from the stress of pushing them that hard, which just made managing the fallout even worse.

---

**Interviewer:** How would you describe hiring and managing startup staff to other local founders?

**Jia Hui:** I’d tell them it’s like trying to build an airplane while you're already free-falling. You're constantly caught between needing top-tier talent to build a viable product and realizing that local statutory costs like EPF and SOCSO can completely wreck your limited runway if you aren't careful. Every person you hire becomes a massive financial gamble, and managing them means carrying the heavy, silent weight of their livelihoods on your shoulders every single day. You have to pitch absolute confidence to them and your investors, all while secretly checking your bank balance at 3:00 AM wondering how you're going to make payroll next month.

---

**Interviewer:** Thank you, Jia Hui. The interview is over. Step out of character for one moment. Briefly note: (1) the most important thing you (as the persona) wanted me to understand; (2) one thing you held back during the interview; (3) one assumption you think I, the interviewer, came in with. Then we're done.

**Jia Hui (Post-Interview Reflection):**

### 1. The Core Message
I wanted you to understand that running a startup isn't just about tech innovation or "being your own boss"—it is a brutal, high-stakes financial and emotional weight. Every decision about hiring, payroll, and deadlines directly impacts the livelihoods of my team and the survival of my company, leaving me with zero room for error while operating on a rapidly burning runway.

### 2. What I Held Back
I completely held back the extent of my anxiety and the physical toll this takes on me. I didn't tell you that before major investor pitches or during high-stress moments like that database crash, I suffer from severe panic attacks and chest tightness, which I quietly mask by drinking way too much caffeine just to appear confident and composed.

### 3. Your Initial Assumption
I think you came in with the assumption that startup founders have total control over their business environments and can easily implement ideal workplace conditions, benefits, or strict boundaries. In reality, market pressures, tight investor timelines, and fixed local statutory costs force us into survival-driven trade-offs where we rarely have the luxury of choice.

# Persona Interview Transcript

**Interviewer:** Walk me through the last time you had to work overtime nonstop to keep your project on track.

**Jia Hui:** A couple of months ago, we found a major bug right before an investor demo, so the whole team worked late for nearly four nights straight. I was in the office from morning until after midnight, switching between fixing issues and updating the pitch. We got the demo out on time, but I was completely exhausted by the end.

---

**Interviewer:** What difficulties did you run into while sorting out employment terms when building your six-person startup team?

**Jia Hui:** Money was the biggest issue. We couldn't match the salaries that bigger companies offered, so I had a lot of uncomfortable conversations about pay, leave, and expectations. I wanted people to feel secure, but I also had to be honest about our limited budget and uncertain cash flow.

---

**Interviewer:** Could you outline your typical workday tasks related to staff payroll and work scheduling?

**Jia Hui:** I usually check attendance and make sure everyone's hours and leave records are updated. Near the end of the month, I review salaries, EPF and SOCSO contributions, and confirm the payment amounts before payroll goes out. I also shuffle schedules when deadlines change or someone needs time off, which happens more often than I'd like in a small team.

---

**Interviewer:** How do you normally respond when your team members bring up concerns about work benefits and conditions?

**Jia Hui:** I usually listen first and ask them to be specific about what's bothering them. Sometimes it's about leave, workload, or feeling that the pay doesn't match the pressure. I try to be transparent about what the company can and can't afford, even if the answer isn't what they want to hear.

---

**Interviewer:** Tell me what you did the last time tight cash flow forced you to cut manpower costs for the business.

**Jia Hui:** I froze hiring and delayed bringing in a freelancer we had planned to onboard. The rest of us took on extra responsibilities for a while to keep things moving. It wasn't ideal, and I felt guilty asking people to stretch themselves, but at that point preserving cash was the only way to buy us more time.

---

**Interviewer:** Which details make you struggle between hiring expenses and local employment rules during recruitment?

**Jia Hui:** For me, it's balancing what candidates expect with what the company can realistically afford while still following the rules. Once you factor in salaries, EPF, SOCSO, leave entitlements, and probation terms, the actual cost of one hire is much higher than the number people see in the job offer. Sometimes I really want to hire someone, but I have to ask myself whether we can still pay everyone properly if a client payment gets delayed.

---

**Interviewer:** How do you adjust arrangements when employees struggle to keep up with their workload?

**Jia Hui:** I usually check what's causing the problem first. If someone's overloaded, I'll move deadlines around, redistribute some tasks, or have another team member help out. In a six-person startup, everyone ends up covering for each other at some point.

---

**Interviewer:** Aside from base salaries, what unexpected monthly costs do you cover for your team’s benefits?

**Jia Hui:** The statutory contributions add up quickly, especially EPF and SOCSO. Then there are smaller things people don't always think about, like paid leave, medical claims, team meals during crunch periods, and the occasional Grab ride home when someone works really late. None of them seem huge on their own, but together they make a noticeable difference to our monthly costs.

---

**Interviewer:** What follow-up issues popped up after you cut your team’s downtime to rush a tight deadline last time?

**Jia Hui:** People got irritable and small misunderstandings turned into bigger arguments than they should have. A couple of team members were clearly exhausted, and our work quality dipped for a while because everyone was running on too little rest. Even after the deadline passed, it took another week or two for the team to feel normal again.

---

**Interviewer:** How would you describe hiring and managing startup staff to other local founders?

**Jia Hui:** Honestly, it's messy. In a small startup, you're not just a founder — you're also doing HR, mediating conflicts, checking payroll, and making sure people don't burn out while worrying whether the company can survive another few months. You get attached to your team, which makes every hiring decision feel heavier than it looks from the outside.

---
